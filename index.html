About Me


I am a member of the Clouds, Aerosol, & Climate group led by Graham Feingold at NOAA Chemical Sciences Laboratory since 2020, first as a National Research Council Fellowship awardee and currently as a research scientist at the Cooperative Institute for Research in Environmental Sciences (CIRES) at the University of Colorado Boulder. I earned my Ph.D. in Meteorology & Physical Oceanography from the University of Miami in 2020, receiving one of eight University of Miami Fellowship awards across the university, under the supervision of Paquita Zuidema

I'm interested in developing innovative data-driven approaches, leveraging observational and modeling datasets of the climate system, to better understand cloud processes, their natural variability and responses to external forcings across scales. In particular, my research has focused extensively on aerosol-cloud interactions, utilizing a diverse set of tools ranging from geostatistical analyses of satellite remote sensing and field measurements to high-fidelity numerical modeling to Artificial Intelligence.

Research Topics

Marine Cloud Brightening & Aerosol-Cloud Interactions

My research focuses extensively on aerosol-cloud interactions in marine environments, with particular emphasis on cloud albedo susceptibility and marine cloud brightening (MCB). I investigate how aerosols influence cloud properties and how these interactions vary across different meteorological conditions and geographical regions. This work has significant implications for understanding climate feedback mechanisms and potential geoengineering approaches.

Related research: Zhang & Feingold (2023), Zhang et al. (2022)

Shipping Emissions & Climate Impact

I study the radiative forcing effects of shipping emissions regulations, particularly the IMO2020 fuel regulation. My research examines how reduced pollution from shipping affects cloud properties and climate, revealing that while these regulations have significant environmental benefits, they also produce a warming effect that is challenging to detect amid natural variability.

Related research: Zhang et al. (2025)

Biomass Burning Aerosol Effects

My work investigates how biomass burning smoke affects marine boundary layer clouds, particularly in the southeast Atlantic region. I've discovered that sunlight-absorbing aerosols from biomass burning amplify the seasonal cycle in low-cloud fraction, with significant implications for regional climate dynamics and radiative effects.

Related research: Zhang & Zuidema (2021), Zhang & Zuidema (2019)

Cloud Process Modeling & Machine Learning

I develop innovative data-driven approaches to understand cloud processes, leveraging large-eddy simulations, satellite observations, and machine learning techniques. My research examines how cloud water adjusts to aerosol perturbations and how these processes are buffered by solar heating in non-precipitating marine stratocumuli.

Related research: Zhang et al. (2024), Chen, Zhang et al. (2024)
